🌺 Why Greenwaste Recycling Matters on the Big Island

Hawaiʻi Island's exceptional biodiversity — including our native ʻōhiʻa lehua forests, lowland koa groves, and coastal naupaka — depends on a healthy ecosystem cycle. When yard waste and food scraps go to the landfill, they decompose without oxygen and produce methane, a potent greenhouse gas. On an island that imports over 85% of its food, rebuilding our agricultural soil through composting is one of the most powerful tools we have for long-term food security and environmental health.

The County of Hawaiʻi operates two dedicated organic material processing facilities — one in East Hawaiʻi (Hilo) and one in West Hawaiʻi (Puʻuanahulu) — transforming your yard waste into nutrient-rich mulch that residents can pick up for free. This keeps valuable organic matter in circulation on our island rather than burying it in a landfill.

For Residents Only — Businesses & Farms Prohibited

County Recycling & Transfer Stations accept greenwaste from residential households only. Businesses, farms, government agencies, and non-profit organizations are prohibited from using these sites for greenwaste disposal. Commercial entities must use the East or West Hawaiʻi Organics Facility directly.

  • ʻŌhiʻa vegetative material is NOT accepted at any County facility — this protects against the spread of Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death (ROD), a devastating fungal disease threatening our native forests.
  • Soil, rocks, and sand are not accepted as greenwaste.
  • All facilities are closed on New Year's Day, Thanksgiving Day, and Christmas Day.
🌳
Protecting Our ʻŌhiʻa Lehua Forests

ʻŌhiʻa lehua (Metrosideros polymorpha) is Hawaiʻi's most iconic and ecologically critical native tree, covering over 800,000 acres across the Big Island. Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death (ROD), caused by the Ceratocystis fungus, has already killed millions of trees since it was detected in 2014.

To prevent accidental spread, no ʻōhiʻa material — including wood, branches, leaves, or bark — may be dropped off at any County greenwaste facility. If you suspect ROD on your property, contact the DLNR Division of Forestry and Wildlife at (808) 974-7210 or report online at rapidohiadeath.org.

Processing centers

Hawaiʻi Organics Facilities

The County operates two dedicated greenwaste composting facilities — one serving East Hawaiʻi and one serving West Hawaiʻi. Both are operated by Hawaiian Earth Recycling and are open to the public for direct drop-off and mulch pick-up.

🌺 East Hawaiʻi

East Hawaiʻi Organics Facility

Located in Hilo, this facility accepts greenwaste from residential and commercial sources in East Hawaiʻi. Material is shredded and processed into high-quality mulch, which is then available for free pick-up by residents on a first-come, first-served basis. The facility processes thousands of tons of organic material annually, diverting it from the Hilo Sanitary Landfill.

At-home solutions

Home Composting & Food Recycling

The best greenwaste is the kind you never have to haul away. Hawaiʻi's warm, humid climate is ideal for home composting — organic material breaks down fast, often within weeks. Combine your yard waste with food scraps and you'll have rich compost soil amendment in no time.

🗑️

Backyard Composting

Hawaiʻi's tropical warmth makes backyard composting one of the most effective waste reduction strategies on the island. Layer green material (grass, food scraps) with brown material (dry leaves, small branches), keep the pile moist, and turn regularly. You'll produce rich compost within 4–8 weeks year-round.

🥗

Food Scraps & Kitchen Waste

Combine your greenwaste with food scraps in your home compost bin for maximum diversion. Accepted food scraps include fruit and vegetable peels, coffee grounds, eggshells, and garden-fresh trimmings. Food recycling is one of the fastest-growing waste reduction strategies in Hawaiʻi County's zero waste plan.

Learn more at Hawaiʻi Zero Waste →
🪱

Vermicomposting (Worm Bins)

Worm bins are perfect for Hawaiʻi apartments and small yards. Red wigglers thrive in our climate and convert kitchen scraps into worm castings — one of the richest natural fertilizers available. Worm bins produce no odor when properly managed and can sit on a lanai or under the kitchen sink.

🎋

Mulching in Place

For yard clippings and leaves, consider leaving them where they fall (grasscycling) or creating a simple mulch layer around your trees and garden beds. This mimics the natural forest floor cycle native to Hawaiʻi's upland forests and keeps moisture in the soil — especially important during dry kona weather patterns.

🌿

Bokashi Fermentation

Bokashi is a Japanese fermentation method that can process all food waste, including meat and dairy — items not suitable for traditional composting. The inoculated bran pickles food waste in an airtight bucket, producing a fermented material that can be buried in the garden or added to a compost pile. Works well in Hawaiʻi's heat.

🌱

Community Gardens & Sharing

Many community gardens and neighborhood farms across the Big Island welcome donated compostable material. Excess yard waste from large properties can also be shared via community boards, Nextdoor, or local Facebook groups — connecting neighbors who need mulch with those who have excess material.

Before you go

Tips for a Smooth Drop-Off

A little preparation goes a long way. These tips help keep the facilities running smoothly and ensure your load is accepted without issue.

📏

Cut It Down to Size

Branches and logs must be under 1 foot in diameter and 3 feet in length for transfer station drop-off. A chainsaw or loppers can make all the difference.

🚫

No ʻŌhiʻa — Ever

Do not bring any ʻōhiʻa material, even if it looks healthy. Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death spreads through infected wood and soil. Protect our forests by leaving ʻōhiʻa at home.

🛻

Secure Your Load

All loads must be covered or secured when transporting on county roads. Loose branches and leaves can blow off and create road hazards — use tarps or netting.

📞

Call Ahead for Mulch

Free mulch availability changes daily. Call Hawaiian Earth Recycling at (808) 935-2277 before making the trip so you don't arrive to find the pile empty.

🧹

Keep Loads Clean

Don't mix regular trash with your greenwaste. Mixed loads may be rejected and you'll need to sort at the gate. Keep a dedicated bin in your truck for green material only.

☀️

Go Early in the Day

Transfer stations can get busy, especially on weekends. Arriving in the morning avoids long lines and ensures the facility attendants have time to assist you properly.

🏘️

Residents Only at Stations

Bring your proof of residency (driver's license with Big Island address) if asked. Commercial and farm greenwaste must go directly to the Organics Facilities.

🌧️

Wet Material Is Fine

Hawaiʻi's frequent rain means your yard waste will often be wet. That's okay — wet greenwaste is actually better for composting. There's no need to let it dry out first.

Frequently Asked Questions

Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death (ROD) is a devastating disease caused by two fungal species (Ceratocystis lukuohia and Ceratocystis huliohia) that kill ʻōhiʻa lehua trees rapidly — often within weeks of infection. Since it was identified on the Big Island in 2014, ROD has killed millions of ʻōhiʻa trees, threatening the ecosystem services that native forests provide, including watershed protection and habitat for endangered Hawaiian birds.

The fungus spreads through infected wood, soil, and equipment. Even seemingly healthy ʻōhiʻa material may carry the spores. For this reason, the County prohibits any ʻōhiʻa material from entering the greenwaste stream. If you have diseased ʻōhiʻa on your property, do not move it — contact DLNR at (808) 974-7210.

The County's greenwaste program primarily handles yard and garden material. For food waste, the County encourages residents to compost at home using backyard bins, worm bins (vermicomposting), or bokashi fermentation systems. These methods are highly effective in Hawaiʻi's climate and produce valuable garden amendments.

Incorporating food scraps into your home compost alongside greenwaste is one of the most impactful things you can do to reduce your household waste footprint. Visit the Hawaiʻi Zero Waste website or contact DEM for resources on getting started with home composting.

You can bring greenwaste in any vehicle — pickup truck, SUV, or car. Many residents use reusable bins, paper yard waste bags, or simply load loose material in their trunk or truck bed. There is no minimum load requirement.

If transporting loose material on county roads, state law requires loads to be covered or secured to prevent debris from blowing off. Tarps, cargo nets, or covered containers are all acceptable. Plastic bags may be used for transport but should be emptied and removed before placing material in the greenwaste bin.

Yes — invasive plant material is generally accepted in the greenwaste program, and removing invasive species from your property is encouraged. The composting process at the Organics Facilities reaches temperatures high enough to kill most invasive plant seeds.

Common invasive plants found on the Big Island that are accepted include: guinea grass, strawberry guava (waiawī), ginger, bamboo, and many ornamental species that have escaped into native habitat. The exception remains ʻōhiʻa — even invasive-appearing wood must not be included if it is ʻōhiʻa or might be ʻōhiʻa.

  • If unsure whether a plant is ʻōhiʻa, do not include it — contact DLNR for identification assistance.
  • For large-scale invasive species removal projects, contact the Big Island Invasive Species Committee (BIISC) at biisc.org.
