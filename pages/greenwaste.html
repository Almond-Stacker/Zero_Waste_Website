The County's Greenwaste Recycling program is simple, free for residents, and available island-wide. Here's how your yard waste makes the journey from your property back to our community's gardens.

Collect yard waste — grass, leaves, palm fronds, banana stalks, branches (under 1 ft diameter / 3 ft length). Keep all ʻōhiʻa material out. Secure your load for transport on county roads.

🌺 Why Greenwaste Recycling Matters on the Big Island

Hawaiʻi Island is one of the most biodiverse places on Earth. Our volcanic landscapes support an extraordinary range of native ecosystems — from the ʻōhiʻa-fern rainforests of Puna to the alpine shrublands of Mauna Kea, the dry leeward forests of Kohala, and the rich coffee slopes of Kona. When yard waste and food scraps go to the landfill, they decompose without oxygen and release methane — a greenhouse gas over 80 times more potent than CO₂ in the short term.

On an island that imports over 85% of its food and faces relentless pressure from invasive species, rebuilding our agricultural soil through composting is one of the most powerful steps we can take toward long-term food security and ecosystem health. The County's greenwaste program keeps valuable organic matter cycling locally — transforming your yard waste into mulch that enriches our young volcanic soils and helps restore the ʻāina.

🌿
Most Endangered Place on Earth Hawaiʻi has more endangered species per square mile than anywhere else. Composting enriches native soils that support this unique biodiversity.
🌋
Young Volcanic Soils Our lava-derived soils are nutrient-poor. Wood chip mulch dramatically improves water retention and biological activity in these young substrates.
🐠
Reef Protection Mulching gardens reduces soil erosion and nutrient runoff that smothers the coral reefs along our coastlines — including the reef at Kealakekua Bay.
🌧️
Hilo's 130+ Inches of Rain In one of the rainiest cities in the U.S., organic mulch is essential for retaining nutrients rather than letting them wash into streams and the ocean.
🌴
Tropical Abundance Banana trees, palm fronds, heliconia, ti plants, and ginger create large volumes of yard waste year-round — all recyclable in this program.
🦋
Local Food Security Every ton of compost produced reduces landfill use and builds the soil health essential for expanding local farming and reducing food imports.
🚫
For Residents Only — Commercial Use Prohibited at Transfer Stations

County Recycling & Transfer Stations accept greenwaste from residential households only. Businesses, farms, government agencies, and non-profit organizations are prohibited from using transfer stations for greenwaste disposal. Commercial entities must use the East or West Hawaiʻi Organics Facilities directly, where a tip fee of $32.00 per ton (pro-rated) applies.

  • Vehicle limit: Type I & Type II vehicles only (under 20,000 lbs GVW; loads under 5 cubic yards). Maximum 1 greenwaste drop-off per vehicle per day. Maximum 5 cubic yards of greenwaste per day per vehicle/customer.
  • ʻŌhiʻa vegetative material is NOT accepted at any County facility — this protects against the spread of Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death (ROD), which has already killed millions of trees on the Big Island.
  • Soil, rocks, and sand are not greenwaste and will not be accepted.
  • All facilities are closed on New Year's Day, Thanksgiving Day, and Christmas Day.
🌳
Protecting Our ʻŌhiʻa Lehua Forests — No Exceptions

ʻŌhiʻa lehua (Metrosideros polymorpha) is Hawaiʻi's most iconic and ecologically critical native tree, covering over 800,000 acres across the Big Island and forming the backbone of our native forest watersheds. Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death (ROD), caused by the Ceratocystis lukuohia and Ceratocystis huliohia fungi, has killed millions of trees since 2014 and continues to spread.

To prevent spread, no ʻōhiʻa material — including wood, branches, leaves, bark, or roots — may be brought to any County greenwaste facility. If you suspect ROD on your property, do not move any material. Contact the DLNR Division of Forestry & Wildlife at (808) 974-7210 or report online at rapidohiadeath.org.

Know before you go

Vehicle & Load Restrictions

To keep the program free and fair for all Hawaiʻi County residents, the following vehicle and load limits apply to residential greenwaste drop-off at County Transfer Stations.

🚛

Accepted Vehicle Types

Type I & Type II vehicles only — vehicles under 20,000 lbs gross vehicle weight. Standard pickup trucks, SUVs, minivans, cars with trailers, and small flatbeds all qualify. Large commercial trucks, dump trucks, and semi-trailers are not permitted at transfer stations for residential drop-off.

📦

Maximum Load Size

5 cubic yards per visit, per vehicle. A standard pickup truck bed holds roughly 1–2 cubic yards. Five cubic yards is approximately 2–3 full truck beds. Loads that exceed this limit at a transfer station will need to go directly to an Organics Facility where larger loads are processed.

📅

Daily Drop-Off Frequency

Maximum 1 greenwaste drop-off per vehicle per day. This limit keeps the program available and fair for all community members. If you have multiple large loads from a major yard project, plan to spread them over multiple days — or haul directly to an Organics Facility for large volumes.

🪪

Residency Requirement

Hawaiʻi County residents only. You may be asked to show a Hawaiʻi driver's license or state ID with a County of Hawaiʻi address. This program is funded by local taxes and is exclusively for the benefit of Big Island residents. Commercial haulers will be turned away from transfer stations.

The sanitization process

How Our Mulch Is Made — & Why It's Safe

The County's mulch isn't just shredded yard waste. It undergoes a carefully controlled composting process designed to neutralize invasive species threats that are uniquely dangerous in Hawaiʻi's fragile ecosystem.

🌿

Collection & Shredding

Greenwaste from all transfer stations is combined and shredded into small chips to maximize the surface area available for microbial composting activity.

🔄

Windrow Turning — Minimum 5 Times

Material is formed into long windrow piles and turned at least 5 times during processing. Turning introduces oxygen and ensures even heating throughout the entire pile.

🌡️

Sustained Heat: 131°F for 15+ Days

The pile must maintain a minimum temperature of 131°F (55°C) for at least 15 consecutive days. This heat destroys harmful bacteria, weed seeds, and invasive pests before any mulch is released to the public.

✅

Public Release

Only after all conditions are met is the mulch approved for pick-up. If the process is still running, mulch may be temporarily unavailable — always call ahead before making the trip.

🛡️ Invasive Threats Eliminated by the Sanitization Process

🪲
Coconut Rhinoceros Beetle (CRB) Devastating pest currently threatening Oʻahu — larvae and eggs are killed at 131°F, preventing spread to the Big Island through mulch.
🐜
Little Fire Ants (LFA) Extremely painful stinging ants already established on the Big Island that threaten farm workers, pets, and native wildlife. Heat destroys eggs and queens.
🐸
Coqui Frogs (Eleutherodactylus coqui) Invasive frogs from Puerto Rico that disrupt native bird habitat and create noise pollution across the island. Eggs are killed at processing temperatures.
🌳
ʻŌhiʻa Wilt Fungus (Ceratocystis) Causes Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death. Heat treatment neutralizes fungal spores — though ʻōhiʻa material is still prohibited as an added precaution.

At-home solutions

Home Composting & Food Recycling

The best greenwaste is the kind you never have to haul away. Hawaiʻi's warm, humid climate is nearly perfect for home composting — organic material breaks down fast, often within weeks. Our year-round growing season means a constant supply of both greenwaste and garden beds that need amendment.

🗑️

Backyard Composting

Hawaiʻi's tropical warmth and humidity create nearly ideal composting conditions. Layer green material (fresh grass clippings, banana leaves, food scraps) with brown material (dry leaves, small branches, cardboard) and keep the pile moist. In the Big Island's climate, you can produce finished compost in as little as 4–8 weeks year-round.

UH-CTAHR Composting Guide for Hawaiʻi →
🥗

Food Scraps & Kitchen Waste

Hawaiʻi is a food lover's paradise — guava, papaya, mango, lychee, breadfruit, lilikoi, and pineapple all generate rich organic scraps perfect for composting. Mixing fruit rinds, coffee grounds, eggshells, and vegetable peels with your greenwaste dramatically speeds up the composting process and produces a richer end product.

Learn more at Hawaiʻi Zero Waste →
🪱

Vermicomposting (Worm Bins)

Red wigglers thrive in Hawaiʻi's warm climate and convert kitchen scraps into worm castings — one of the richest natural fertilizers available. Worm bins produce no odor when properly maintained and can live on the lanai, under the kitchen sink, or in a shaded corner of your garden. Ideal for apartments in Kailua-Kona or Hilo.

🎋

Grasscycling & Mulching In Place

Leave grass clippings where they fall. In Hawaiʻi's fast-growing lawns, clippings decompose within days and return nitrogen directly to the soil — reducing your need for synthetic fertilizer. Layer fallen ti leaves, plumeria blossoms, and banana leaves around trees and shrubs to mimic the natural forest floor cycle of native Hawaiʻi ecosystems.

🌿

Bokashi Fermentation

Bokashi is a Japanese fermentation method that can process all food waste including meat and dairy — items not suitable for traditional composting. It works especially well in Hawaiʻi's heat. The fermented material can be buried directly in garden beds or added to a compost pile, breaking down completely in just a few weeks in our tropical soils.

🌱

Community Gardens & Sharing

Many community gardens, taro farms, and neighborhood farms across the Big Island welcome donated organic material. Residents with large properties can connect with neighbors who need mulch through Nextdoor, local Facebook groups, or the Hawaiʻi Community Garden Network — keeping organic matter local and reducing hauling trips for everyone.

🌿 Hawaiʻi's Invasive Species Problem & How You Can Help

Hawaiʻi faces one of the world's most severe invasive species crises. Many common yard plants — strawberry guava (waiawī), miconia, fountain grass, ginger, and Christmas berry — aggressively crowd out native vegetation and degrade native bird habitat. The good news: the County's greenwaste program actively accepts invasive plant material. Bringing in invasive species for composting is one of the most direct environmental contributions you can make as a Big Island resident.

The composting process reaches 131°F for a minimum of 15 days — sufficient to kill most invasive plant seeds and vegetative propagules before the finished mulch is returned to the public. The only strict exception remains ʻōhiʻa lehua, which is prohibited at all County facilities due to the risk of spreading Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death. When in doubt about any plant — leave it out and contact DLNR for identification.

Before you go

Tips for a Smooth Drop-Off

A little preparation goes a long way. These tips help keep County facilities running efficiently and ensure your load is accepted without delay.

📏

Cut It Down to Size

Branches and logs must be under 1 foot in diameter and 3 feet in length for transfer station drop-off. A chainsaw or loppers makes the difference between an accepted and rejected load at the gate.

🚫

No ʻŌhiʻa — Ever

Do not bring any ʻōhiʻa material, even if it looks completely healthy. Rapid ʻŌhiʻa Death spreads invisibly through wood and soil. A single infected branch can devastate an entire forest stand.

🛻

Secure Your Load

Hawaiʻi state law requires all loads to be covered or secured when transporting on public roads. Loose branches and leaves blowing off create road hazards — use tarps, cargo nets, or covered containers.

📞

Call Ahead for Mulch

Mulch availability changes daily and may be temporarily unavailable while the 131°F/15-day sanitization process completes. Call (808) 935-2277 before making the trip so you don't arrive empty-handed.

🧹

Keep Loads Clean

Don't mix household trash with your greenwaste. Mixed loads may be rejected entirely at the gate. Keep a dedicated bin or tarp in your vehicle exclusively for green yard material.

☀️

Go Early in the Day

Transfer stations get busy, especially on weekday afternoons and weekends. Arriving in the morning avoids long wait times and gives facility staff more time to assist you if needed.

🪪

Bring Your Hawaiʻi ID

Bring a valid Hawaiʻi driver's license or state ID showing a County of Hawaiʻi address. Commercial haulers will be turned away at transfer stations — residential customers only.

🌧️

Wet Material Is Fine

In one of the rainiest places on Earth, wet greenwaste is the norm. Wet material is actually better for composting — no need to wait for it to dry out before dropping off at the station.

🔴

Watch for Little Fire Ants

Little Fire Ants may be present in yard debris from affected areas of the island. Wear gloves when loading material. The composting heat process kills LFA, but protect yourself during collection and transport.

If you suspect Little Fire Ants are present in your yard debris, take precautions during collection and transport: wear gloves and long sleeves, avoid handling material bare-handed, and cover your load securely. If you find an LFA infestation on your property, report it to the Hawaiʻi Department of Agriculture and contact the LFA Working Group for treatment assistance at lfa.hawaii.gov.
