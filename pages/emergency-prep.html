Preparedness & Safety

Emergency Preparations

Hawaiʻi Island faces unique natural hazards — from hurricanes and tsunamis to lava flows and flooding. Learn how to prepare, protect your household, and manage waste safely before, during, and after an emergency.

🚨
During an Active Emergency

Follow all instructions from Hawaiʻi County Civil Defense and emergency responders. Do not attempt to dispose of hazardous or debris waste until authorities have declared it safe to do so. For current alerts, visit hawaiicounty.gov/civil-defense or call (808) 935-0031.

Know your risks

Hawaiʻi Island Hazard Guide

Select a hazard type below to see specific waste management and safety guidance for that event.

📦

Before the Storm — Secure & Prep

  • Bring in all loose items — outdoor furniture, trash cans, recycling bins, and yard decorations become dangerous projectiles in high winds.
  • Secure hazardous materials — store chemicals, pesticides, and motor oil indoors or in a sealed, elevated location away from flood paths.
  • Prepare your rubbish bins — place lids securely and if your pickup day falls during a storm, set bins out only once the storm has passed and it's safe to do so.
  • Clear gutters and drains — remove green waste, leaves, and debris that could block drainage and worsen flooding.
  • Store go-bag supplies — include a 3-day supply of water (1 gallon per person per day), non-perishable food, medications, and important documents.
🧹

After the Storm — Cleanup & Disposal

  • Wait for the all-clear — do not begin cleanup until officials confirm it is safe. Downed power lines and unstable structures are life-threatening.
  • Separate debris types — keep green waste, construction debris, appliances, and regular rubbish in separate piles for efficient collection.
  • Do not burn debris — open burning of storm debris is prohibited in Hawaiʻi County without a permit and creates toxic smoke.
  • Flood-damaged appliances — large appliances soaked by floodwater may contain mold and hazardous materials. Place separately for bulk pickup collection.
  • Food safety — discard any food that has been exposed to floodwater or has been unrefrigerated for more than 4 hours during a power outage.
  • Monitor DEM announcements — the County may activate special debris collection programs. Sign up for alerts at hawaiicounty.gov.
🏠

Evacuation — What to Take

  • Medications and medical equipment — take at least a 2-week supply if possible.
  • Important documents — IDs, insurance papers, bank records, and property deeds in a waterproof bag or digital backup.
  • Leave hazardous materials behind — do not attempt to move propane tanks, chemical containers, or vehicle fluids during an emergency evacuation.
  • Pets and livestock — plan ahead with a designated location and enough food and water for 72 hours minimum.
  • Go early — lava can move faster than expected and roads may become impassable. Evacuate as soon as a warning is issued.
☣️

Lava-Damaged Property & Waste

  • Asbestos and hazardous materials — older homes destroyed by lava may release asbestos fibers and other contaminants. Do not disturb without professional assessment.
  • Laze hazard — lava entering the ocean creates toxic "laze" (lava haze) containing hydrochloric acid and fine glass particles. Stay well away.
  • Propane tanks — do not attempt to recover propane tanks or fuel containers near active lava. Alert emergency responders.
  • Abandoned vehicles — vehicles abandoned due to lava flows may contain fuel, batteries, and fluids. Contact the County for guidance on abandoned vehicle removal.
  • Post-eruption cleanup — contact the Hawaiʻi Department of Health and County DEM before beginning any major cleanup on lava-affected property.
🏃

Immediate Response — Evacuate

  • Do not return to collect belongings — your safety is the only priority. Multiple waves can arrive over hours.
  • Move inland and uphill immediately — the Hawaiʻi Tsunami Evacuation Zone maps are posted at hawaiicounty.gov. Know your zone before an event.
  • Coastal contamination — tsunami waters carry debris, sewage, and chemical contaminants from inland areas. Avoid contact with floodwater.
  • Wait for the all-clear from Civil Defense — do not return to the inundation zone until authorities confirm it is safe.
🗑️

Post-Tsunami Debris & Cleanup

  • Marine debris — tsunami debris may include hazardous marine life, sharp metal, and contaminated materials. Always wear gloves and protective footwear.
  • Contaminated soil and sediment — floodwater can deposit toxic sediment. Do not allow children to play in affected areas until officially cleared.
  • Flood-damaged appliances & electronics — saltwater-soaked appliances and electronics must be properly disposed of — they cannot be recycled through standard programs. Contact DEM for guidance.
  • Separate your debris — use separate piles for wood/organic material, metals, appliances, and regular rubbish to speed up county collection.
  • Report hazardous waste — call the State of Hawaiʻi Department of Health at (808) 586-4249 for large-scale hazardous material releases.
⚡

During Heavy Rain & Flooding

  • Never enter floodwater on foot or by car — just 6 inches of moving water can knock a person down; 12 inches can carry away a vehicle.
  • Move chemicals and hazardous waste uphill — if you can do so safely, move pesticides, motor oil, and cleaning chemicals off the ground floor before flooding occurs.
  • Do not dump anything in drainage channels — during flooding, storm drains flow directly to our ocean and streams. Never dump waste, chemicals, or debris into drains.
  • Protect your septic system — avoid using toilets and sinks if you suspect your septic system is flooded, which can cause sewage backup and contamination.
🧼

Cleanup After Flooding

  • Document before you remove — photograph all damage before beginning cleanup for insurance and disaster assistance purposes.
  • Flood-soaked drywall and insulation — porous materials that absorbed floodwater must be removed and disposed of — they cannot be dried and reused due to mold and contamination risk.
  • Mold begins within 24–48 hours — remove wet materials quickly and use fans and dehumidifiers to dry out spaces. Wear N95 or better respiratory protection.
  • Flood-damaged HHW — household chemicals, paints, and pesticides contaminated by floodwater become hazardous waste. Keep containers sealed and take to the next HHW Collection Event.
  • Green waste from flooding — downed trees and branches can be taken to any County Transfer Station for green waste disposal.
😷

Protecting Yourself from Vog

  • What is vog? — Volcanic smog (vog) consists of sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and fine particle pollution released by Kīlauea and other Hawaiʻi volcanoes. It can irritate eyes, throat, and lungs.
  • Stay indoors on high-vog days — check the Vog Measurement and Prediction Project (VMAP) dashboard at vmap.soest.hawaii.edu for daily forecasts.
  • Do not burn waste or open-burn on high-vog days — burning waste worsens air quality and is dangerous for those with respiratory conditions.
  • Use an N95 respirator — standard dust masks do not filter SO₂ gas. Use an N95 or better to filter fine particles outdoors.
  • People at risk — those with asthma, heart disease, young children, and elderly individuals are especially vulnerable during elevated vog events.
🌿

Laze — Ocean Entry Hazard

  • What is laze? — When lava enters the ocean, it creates laze — a toxic plume of hydrochloric acid gas (HCl) and tiny glass shards (Pele's hair). It is extremely hazardous.
  • Stay upwind and at a safe distance — remain at least 300 meters away from any active lava ocean entry. Wind can shift rapidly, bringing laze toward you without warning.
  • Do not swim near entry points — lava benches are unstable and can collapse suddenly. The surrounding ocean is acidic and contains suspended glass particles.
  • No respiratory protection is sufficient for laze — the only safe action is to maintain significant distance and stay upwind at all times.

Be ready

Emergency Supply Checklist

Prepare at least a 72-hour emergency kit for every member of your household — including pets. Hawaiʻi Island's isolation means supplies can take longer to arrive after a disaster.

💧

Water & Food

  • 1 gallon of water per person per day (3-day minimum)
  • Non-perishable food for 3 days minimum
  • Manual can opener
  • Infant formula and baby food if applicable
  • Pet food and water for animals
  • Water purification tablets or filter
💊

Medical & Safety

  • Prescription medications (2-week supply if possible)
  • First-aid kit with manual
  • N95 or better respirators for vog and ash
  • Extra eyeglasses or contact lens supplies
  • Hearing aids with extra batteries
  • Hand sanitizer and disinfecting wipes
🔦

Power & Communications

  • Battery-powered or hand-crank NOAA weather radio
  • Flashlights with extra batteries
  • Portable phone charger / power bank
  • Extra batteries in multiple sizes
  • Candles and waterproof matches
  • Whistle or signaling device
📄

Documents & Finances

  • Copies of IDs, passports, and birth certificates
  • Insurance cards and policy documents
  • Bank account and financial records
  • Small amount of cash (ATMs may be unavailable)
  • Emergency contact list (written, not just digital)
  • Medications list and medical records
🧰

Tools & Sanitation

  • Multi-tool or basic tool set
  • Heavy-duty gloves (for cleanup)
  • Sturdy closed-toe shoes in kit
  • Sanitation supplies (toilet paper, garbage bags)
  • Bucket with tight lid (emergency sanitation)
  • Tarps and rope or bungee cords
🌺

Hawaiʻi-Specific Extras

  • Vog mask / N95 for volcanic smog events
  • Sunscreen and insect repellent
  • Wet/dry weather gear (Hawaiʻi weather is unpredictable)
  • Evacuation zone maps (printed from County website)
  • Tsunami siren awareness — know your nearest sirens
  • Local Civil Defense radio frequency saved to radio

After the event

Disaster Waste — How to Dispose Properly

Different types of disaster waste require different handling. Mixing waste types together slows County collection and can create additional hazards.

🌿

Green Waste & Vegetative Debris

Tree branches, leaves, palm fronds, and grass are the most common hurricane debris on Hawaiʻi Island. Keep separate from other debris types.

  1. 1Cut branches to manageable lengths (4–5 ft where possible)
  2. 2Bundle or place in a separate pile at the curb
  3. 3Drop off at any County Transfer Station (green waste is accepted free of charge)
  4. 4Do not mix with regular rubbish, construction debris, or appliances
🏗️

Construction & Demolition Debris

Damaged lumber, drywall, fencing, roofing materials, and concrete from storm or flood damage. Large volumes require special handling.

  1. 1Remove nails and sharp objects from loose lumber before piling
  2. 2Place in a separate pile from green waste and rubbish
  3. 3Haul to a County Transfer Station accepting C&D debris
  4. 4For large volumes, contact a licensed C&D hauler — the County may announce special collection programs after declared disasters
🧪

Household Hazardous Waste (HHW)

Paints, pesticides, chemicals, motor oil, batteries, and propane tanks contaminated or damaged by a disaster become hazardous waste requiring special disposal.

  1. 1Keep all containers sealed — never pour chemicals down a drain or into the ground
  2. 2Store upright in a cool, well-ventilated area away from floodwater
  3. 3Bring to a scheduled Household Hazardous Waste Collection Event (see DEM website for dates)
  4. 4For large or unknown chemical spills, call State DOH Hazardous Waste: (808) 586-4249
🧲

Appliances & White Goods

Refrigerators, washing machines, air conditioners, and water heaters damaged by floods or storms contain refrigerants and other hazardous materials requiring certified handling.

  1. 1Place appliances at the curb separately from other debris
  2. 2Do not crush, break open, or burn appliances — refrigerant gases are harmful
  3. 3Contact County DEM or your rubbish hauler to schedule a bulk pickup
  4. 4Flood-damaged appliances in working condition may be donated — contact local reuse centers to confirm they can accept storm-affected goods
💻

Electronics & E-Waste

Flood or storm-damaged televisions, computers, and electronics cannot go in your regular rubbish and may not qualify for standard free recycling programs if heavily damaged.

  1. 1Set aside separately — do not place in rubbish bins
  2. 2Contact drop-off locations (Mr. K's Recycle in Hilo or Kona) to confirm they accept disaster-damaged devices
  3. 3Bring to a County HHW or special collection event if electronics are heavily contaminated
  4. 4See the Electronics Recycling page for all drop-off locations and guidelines
🗑️

Regular Rubbish & Food Waste

After a power outage, spoiled food becomes a significant waste issue. Manage it carefully to avoid pest problems and odors.

  1. 1Discard spoiled food in sealed plastic bags before placing in your rubbish bin
  2. 2Keep rubbish bins tightly lidded to deter pests — mongooses, rats, and feral pigs are common in Hawaiʻi
  3. 3Use your regular curbside collection once service resumes — DEM will notify residents of any schedule changes
  4. 4Avoid placing regular rubbish out for collection during active storm conditions

Critical reminders

What You Should and Should Not Do

These simple rules protect your safety, your community's environment, and speed up recovery for everyone.

✅

Do These Things

  • ✓
    Separate debris types — green waste, appliances, C&D debris, and rubbish in distinct piles for faster County collection.
  • ✓
    Wear protective gear — heavy-duty gloves, closed-toe shoes, and N95 masks during debris cleanup.
  • ✓
    Document damage before cleanup — photographs and videos are essential for insurance claims and FEMA disaster assistance.
  • ✓
    Keep HHW sealed — store damaged chemical containers sealed and upright until you can bring them to a collection event.
  • ✓
    Follow official guidance — monitor Civil Defense alerts and DEM announcements for collection schedule updates.
  • ✓
    Help elderly and disabled neighbors — check on those who may need assistance preparing or cleaning up after an emergency.
🚫

Do Not Do These Things

  • ✗
    Do not burn debris — open burning of storm or disaster debris without a permit is prohibited and creates toxic smoke harmful to your community.
  • ✗
    Do not dump into drains, streams, or the ocean — storm drains connect directly to our waterways. Illegal dumping is a crime and harms coral reefs.
  • ✗
    Do not pour chemicals down the drain — this contaminates groundwater and wastewater systems. Always take HHW to a collection event.
  • ✗
    Do not use a generator indoors — carbon monoxide from generators is odorless and deadly. Always operate outdoors with good ventilation.
  • ✗
    Do not enter floodwater — floodwater may be electrically charged from downed power lines and is contaminated with sewage and chemicals.
  • ✗
    Do not assume tap water is safe — after flooding, boil all water until your water utility confirms the system is clear.

Common questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about waste management during and after emergencies on Hawaiʻi Island.

Collection schedules may be suspended, delayed, or modified after major emergencies. The County of Hawaiʻi DEM will communicate any changes through official channels including the County website, social media, and local radio. During an active storm watch or warning, do not place bins at the curb — wait for the event to pass and for official confirmation that collection has resumed.

After a declared disaster, the County may also activate special curbside debris collection programs. Watch for announcements from DEM and your rubbish hauler.

Flood-damaged appliances such as refrigerators, freezers, air conditioners, and water heaters should be placed separately at the curb. Contact your rubbish hauler or the County DEM to schedule a bulk item pickup. These appliances may contain refrigerants that must be recovered by a certified technician — do not attempt to cut, crush, or dismantle them yourself.

If the County has activated a disaster debris collection program following a declared emergency, watch for announcements about bulk pickup schedules in your area.

After any flooding event, you should assume your tap water may be contaminated until your water utility issues a boil water notice clearance. Floodwater can enter municipal water systems and private wells, introducing bacteria, viruses, and chemical contaminants.

  • Boil all water used for drinking, cooking, and brushing teeth until the all-clear is issued
  • If you rely on a private well, have it tested by a certified lab before resuming use
  • Check with the County of Hawaiʻi Department of Water Supply for current advisories
  • Use bottled water from your emergency kit as your primary source in the meantime

Open burning of storm debris, including green waste and construction material, is generally prohibited in Hawaiʻi County without an appropriate permit. Burning treated lumber, plastics, or painted materials releases toxic dioxins and heavy metals that are harmful to your health and the community.

Even with a permit, burning is prohibited on days when air quality is already degraded by vog. Take green waste to a County Transfer Station, and contact the Hawaiʻi County Planning Department regarding any burning permits that may be available during declared disaster recovery periods.

Medications that have been contaminated by floodwater or have exceeded their expiration date should be disposed of safely. Do not flush medications down the toilet or drain — pharmaceuticals can pass through water treatment systems and harm marine ecosystems.

  • Check with your local pharmacy — many participate in medication take-back programs
  • The DEA National Prescription Drug Take Back program has authorized collection sites on Hawaiʻi Island
  • If no take-back option is available, mix medications with coffee grounds or dirt in a sealed bag before placing in the rubbish
  • Medical sharps (needles, lancets) must never go in the rubbish — use a puncture-resistant container and take to a participating pharmacy or health clinic for proper disposal

Illegal dumping of disaster debris — including on roadsides, in streams, or on vacant land — is a serious environmental violation in Hawaiʻi. It can result in fines and criminal prosecution under Hawaiʻi Revised Statutes § 342H.

To report illegal dumping, you can use the County's online reporting form, call the DEM directly, or use the Report Illegal Dumping page on this website. Please provide as much detail as possible — location, description, and photos if safely obtainable.
