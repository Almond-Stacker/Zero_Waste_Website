Why Proper Disposal Matters on an Island

Unlike communities on the continental United States, Hawai'i has no road connections for shipping materials off-island. Improperly discarded appliances, vehicles, and scrap metal can leach oil, refrigerants, heavy metals, and battery acid directly into our groundwater and coral reef ecosystems. Recycling correctly is not just convenient — it's essential for protecting Hawai'i's unique natural environment for future generations.

Appliances & White Goods

Large household appliances — refrigerators, stoves, washers, dryers, water heaters, and more.

Large household appliances — commonly called "white goods" — contain valuable recoverable steel, copper, and aluminum, but may also contain hazardous substances like chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), refrigerants, compressor oil, and mercury switches that require special handling before recycling.

The County of Hawai'i accepts large appliances at all six transfer stations across Hawai'i Island. Appliances that still contain refrigerants (such as refrigerators, freezers, air conditioners, and dehumidifiers) must have their refrigerants properly recovered before they can be recycled. The County facilitates refrigerant recovery on-site at no additional charge.

Planning a large cleanout? The County's Bulky Item Pickup program serves residential customers in most districts — call your local transfer station to schedule a pickup for multiple large appliances.

⚠️
Refrigerant Notice — Required by Federal Law

Refrigerants (Freon, R-22, R-410A) in appliances are federally regulated under the Clean Air Act. It is illegal to release refrigerants into the atmosphere. Do NOT puncture or dismantle a refrigerator, freezer, AC unit, or dehumidifier before drop-off. County staff will handle refrigerant recovery safely using certified EPA-approved equipment.

Automobiles & Vehicles

End-of-life cars, trucks, motorcycles, boats, and other motor vehicles.

With Hawai'i Island's rural distances and aging vehicle fleet, end-of-life vehicles (ELVs) are a significant portion of the County's waste stream. Abandoned vehicles are not just an eyesore — they leak engine oil, transmission fluid, coolant, and battery acid that can contaminate the island's volcanic soil and reach the ocean table.

The good news: an old vehicle is nearly 80% recyclable by weight. The steel, iron, aluminum, copper wiring, and catalytic converter metals can all be recovered and reused. Hawai'i County works with licensed auto recyclers and the State's End-of-Life Vehicle (ELV) Disposal Program to ensure vehicles are depolluted and dismantled responsibly.

Abandoned vehicles on public or private property can be reported to the County for removal through the Abandoned Vehicle Program. Penalties apply for illegal abandonment of vehicles in Hawai'i (HRS §290-11).

📋
Title & Registration Required

To legally transfer or dispose of a vehicle in Hawai'i, you must hold a clear title in your name. Vehicles with liens, missing titles, or out-of-state registration require special handling. Contact the Hawai'i County DMV at (808) 961-8351 or visit hawaiicounty.gov to address title issues before attempting disposal.

Reporting Abandoned Vehicles on Hawai'i Island

If you see a vehicle that has been abandoned on public property for more than 24 hours, you can report it to the Hawai'i Police Department (non-emergency line: (808) 935-3311) or contact the County's Department of Public Works. Abandoned vehicles on private property require a written complaint from the property owner. Under HRS §290, the owner of an abandoned vehicle may be fined up to $500 per day the vehicle remains on public property.

Need help arranging vehicle disposal? The County DEM can connect you with certified auto recyclers and answer program questions.

Scrap Metal

Ferrous and non-ferrous metals from homes, businesses, farms, and construction sites.

Scrap metal is one of the most straightforwardly recyclable materials in the waste stream — virtually all metals can be melted down and remanufactured into new products indefinitely without any loss of quality. On an isolated island like Hawai'i, where everything must be shipped in or out, keeping metal in circulation is especially critical for reducing costs and environmental impact.

Metals fall into two broad categories: ferrous metals (contain iron — steel, cast iron, wrought iron) and non-ferrous metals (aluminum, copper, brass, bronze, stainless steel, lead, and zinc). Non-ferrous metals are generally more valuable and are purchased at higher rates by scrap dealers.

The County accepts scrap metal at all transfer stations at no charge for residential quantities. Commercial haulers should contact DEM for current rates. Several licensed scrap dealers on the island will also purchase clean scrap metal directly, potentially putting money in your pocket.

🔍 Know Your Metal Types
🧲 Ferrous Metals (Magnetic)
  • ✓ Steel beams, pipes, and rebar
  • ✓ Cast iron (pots, engine blocks)
  • ✓ Sheet metal and car bodies
  • ✓ Metal shelving, racks, and cages
  • ✓ Farm equipment and tools
  • ✓ Chain-link fencing

Tip: If a magnet sticks to it, it's probably ferrous.

💎 Non-Ferrous Metals (Higher Value)
  • ✓ Aluminum (cans, siding, gutters, wire)
  • ✓ Copper wire, pipes, and fittings
  • ✓ Brass (valves, faucets, fixtures)
  • ✓ Stainless steel (cookware, sinks)
  • ✓ Lead (old pipes, wheel weights)
  • ✓ Bronze hardware and decorative items

Non-ferrous metals often command $0.30–$4.00+/lb at scrap dealers.

✓

Common Accepted Scrap Items

  • Metal pipes, conduit, and fittings
  • Corrugated metal roofing panels
  • Old tools and hardware
  • Metal furniture (chairs, tables, bed frames)
  • Lawnmower decks and blades
  • Fencing and metal posts
  • Aluminum window frames and doors
  • Copper plumbing and wiring
  • Old water tanks and pressure vessels
  • Metal buckets, drums, and containers (cleaned)
  • Cast iron cookware
  • Boat propellers and hardware
  • Agricultural irrigation pipes
  • Old BBQ grills (cleaned)
  • Steel I-beams and angle iron
✗

Not Accepted as Scrap Metal

  • Radioactive metals or materials
  • Sealed pressurized containers (propane tanks, aerosols)
  • Containers with hazardous residue or chemicals
  • Metal mixed with concrete (unless separated)
  • Scrap tainted with asbestos wrapping
  • Mercury-containing devices (thermometers, switches)
  • Spent ammunition, casings, or firearms (contact HPD)
  • Mixed debris loads with significant non-metal content
⚖️
Scrap Metal Theft is a Felony

Hawai'i law (HRS §708-830.5) prohibits theft of utility infrastructure, irrigation systems, and public property. Licensed dealers are required to record seller ID and hold purchased scrap for a period before resale.

📍 Where to Take Scrap Metal

  • Hilo Scrap & Salvage — Purchases ferrous and non-ferrous. Call for current prices: contact DEM for referral.

Scrap dealers pay market prices and may offer pickup for large quantities. Always bring valid ID — dealers are required by law to record seller information.

💡 Preparation Tips
  • Sort ferrous and non-ferrous metals separately — it speeds processing and may get you a better price at dealers
  • Remove non-metal components (plastic handles, rubber gaskets, wood) where possible
  • Clean containers of any residue before drop-off
  • Cut or flatten large pieces if possible to save space during transport
  • Bring a tarp or liner for your truck bed — metal pieces can cause damage and leave oil stains
  • Bundle wire and conduit to prevent tangling
  • Keep copper, brass, and aluminum separate from steel for maximum value
  • Do not mix your scrap with electronics — those go to the electronics recycling program

Agricultural Scrap Metal — Farm Equipment & Irrigation

Hawai'i Island's farming communities in Hamakua, Ka'ū, and Puna often accumulate large quantities of agricultural scrap metal — old irrigation pipes, sugarcane equipment relics, fencing, and rusted farm machinery. The County encourages farms to coordinate with DEM for large-volume agricultural scrap removal. Some historical plantation-era equipment may qualify for preservation programs — contact the Lyman Museum or Bishop Museum before scrapping unique artifacts.
