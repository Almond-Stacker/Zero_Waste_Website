The Zero Waste Hierarchy

Not all waste management strategies are equal. The hierarchy ranks options from most preferred (top) to least preferred (bottom). Reduce is always first — the waste that never exists never has to be managed.

🚫

Practical action

Top 10 Ways to Reduce Waste

These habits are easy to start, powerful at scale, and save money. Pick one this week — then build from there.

01
Bring Your Own Bag

Keep reusable bags in your car and by the door. One reusable bag replaces hundreds of plastic bags over its lifetime.

02
Carry a Reusable Water Bottle

Americans throw away 50 billion plastic bottles per year. A stainless steel or glass bottle pays for itself in weeks.

03
Say No to Single-Use Utensils

Keep a set of silverware, a cloth napkin, and a reusable straw in your bag or desk drawer for on-the-go meals.

04
Buy in Bulk

Purchasing in larger quantities — or from bulk bins — dramatically reduces packaging waste per unit of product.

05
Choose Products with Less Packaging

Read labels. Opt for items packaged in glass, metal, or cardboard over plastic. Avoid multi-layered or blister packaging.

06
Eat What You Buy

Food waste accounts for nearly 20% of landfill material. Plan meals, store food properly, and compost what you can't use.

07
Go Paperless

Switch to digital billing, receipts, and subscriptions. Unsubscribe from junk mail at DMAchoice.org to stop unwanted catalogs.

08
Repair Before Replacing

A broken zipper, cracked phone screen, or worn shoe sole can often be repaired for far less than replacement — and keeps the item out of the landfill.

09
Borrow or Rent What You Use Rarely

Power tools, party supplies, camping gear — if you'll use it once or twice a year, consider borrowing from a neighbor or renting locally.

10
Opt for Quality Over Quantity

One well-made item that lasts a decade creates less waste than ten cheap replacements. Buy once, buy well — and pass it on when you're done.

County law

Local Ordinances That Help

Hawai'i County has enacted two landmark ordinances to reduce single-use plastics and polystyrene — two of the most harmful and persistent pollutants in our ocean and landscape.

🛍️

Plastic Bag Ordinance

Bill 113 — Effective July 1, 2012

Hawai'i County prohibits retailers from providing single-use plastic checkout bags at the point of sale. Hawai'i was the first county in the nation to ban plastic bags — a milestone in ocean conservation.

Applies to all retail establishments, restaurants, and food vendors
Reusable bags and recyclable paper bags are permitted alternatives
Exemptions for produce bags, dry-cleaning bags, and newspaper bags
Fines range from $100 to $1,000 per violation for repeat offenders

🥡

Polystyrene Ordinance

Bill 59 — Effective January 1, 2020

Polystyrene foam (Styrofoam) food service containers are banned countywide. The lightweight material breaks apart easily, persists for centuries, and is regularly found polluting our beaches and reef systems.

Bans foam plates, cups, bowls, clamshells, and takeout containers
Applies to restaurants, food trucks, farmers markets, and caterers
Compostable and recyclable containers are preferred alternatives
Fines range from $500 to $2,000 per violation depending on frequency
