Specialty Item Drop-Off

Tires, lead-acid batteries, and used cooking oil require special handling — they cannot go in regular trash or recycling. The County of Hawaiʻi operates dedicated drop-off programs for each of these materials at transfer stations across the island.

Passenger & Light Truck Tires

Old tires are accepted at all County transfer stations for a small fee. Limit of 4 tires per day for residential drop-off. Tires must be off the rim.

Used tires cannot be legally landfilled in Hawaiʻi. The County operates a tire recycling program at all transfer stations, accepting passenger, light truck, and medium-duty tires for a modest per-tire disposal fee. Fees help offset transportation and processing costs for shipping tires off-island.

Tires that are accepted are shredded or crumbled into tire-derived aggregate and crumb rubber, which is then used in playground surfaces, athletic tracks, road construction, and other applications.

Tires must be removed from rims before drop-off. If your tires are still mounted on rims, many local tire shops will dismount them for a small fee, or you may discard the tires at the transfer station and keep the rims for the scrap metal program.

Accepted tires are consolidated and shipped off-island to licensed tire recycling facilities. They are shredded or crumbled into:

  • Road construction aggregate
  • Playground safety surfaces

Lead-Acid Batteries

Lead-acid batteries are accepted FREE of charge at all County transfer stations. Never put them in the trash — lead and sulfuric acid are hazardous to our groundwater and reef ecosystems.

Lead-acid batteries — the type used in cars, trucks, motorcycles, boats, and golf carts — are among the most recycled products in the United States. Nearly 99% of the materials in a lead-acid battery can be recovered and reused. The lead is melted and recast, the plastic case is granulated and remanufactured, and the sulfuric acid is neutralized or reclaimed.

Despite their high recyclability, lead-acid batteries remain hazardous if improperly discarded. Lead is a potent neurotoxin, and the sulfuric acid electrolyte can contaminate soil and drinking water. In Hawaiʻi, disposing of lead-acid batteries in regular trash or the landfill is prohibited by State law.

Drop-off is completely free at every County transfer station. Most auto parts retailers (AutoZone, O'Reilly, NAPA) also accept used batteries for free.

✓

Accepted — Lead-Acid Batteries

  • Automotive / car batteries (12V)
  • Truck and heavy equipment batteries
  • Motorcycle and moped batteries
  • Marine / boat batteries
  • Golf cart battery packs
  • RV / camper batteries
  • UPS (uninterruptible power supply) batteries
  • Lawn tractor and riding mower batteries
  • Wheelchair and mobility scooter batteries
✗

Not Accepted Here — Other Batteries

  • Lithium-ion batteries (laptop, phone, power tool) — use HHW or electronics recycler
  • NiMH / NiCd rechargeable batteries — use HHW program
  • Alkaline AA/AAA/C/D batteries — use HHW program
  • Button / coin cell batteries — use HHW program
  • Lithium primary batteries (non-rechargeable) — use HHW
⚠️Safe Handling Tips
  • Always transport upright — never on their side
  • Wear gloves — acid residue can burn skin
  • If cracked or leaking, place in a plastic bag or bin
  • Keep away from sparks or open flames
  • Avoid contact with eyes — wash immediately if splashed
  • Do not store indoors in living areas
🏪Other Drop-Off Options
  • AutoZone — all island locations
  • O'Reilly Auto Parts — Hilo & Kona
  • NAPA Auto Parts — island-wide
  • Walmart Auto Center — Hilo & Kona
  • Costco Tire Center — Kona
  • Most battery retailers accept returns
♻️Why It Matters

A single car battery contains approximately 18 lbs of lead. When recycled, that lead is recovered and reused in new batteries. Recycled lead-acid batteries supply over 80% of the raw lead used in new battery manufacturing — making this one of the most circular material loops in the economy.

💡
Core charge refunds at retailers

When you buy a replacement battery at most auto parts stores, a "core charge" (typically $12–$22) is added to your price. Return your old battery to the same store and the core charge is refunded in full. This is the easiest and most convenient way to recycle your old car battery.

Used Cooking Oil & Grease

Used vegetable cooking oil is accepted free of charge at select transfer stations. Oil must be sealed in clean containers and free of water contamination. It is converted into biodiesel fuel.

Used cooking oil — the vegetable oil leftover from frying at home or in restaurants — is a valuable resource that can be converted into biodiesel fuel. Rather than pouring it down the drain (which clogs pipes and pollutes waterways) or throwing it in the trash (where it can leach into landfill), the County accepts it at select transfer stations for recycling.

The program accepts used vegetable-based cooking oils: canola, sunflower, soybean, corn, coconut, and similar oils. Once collected, the oil is processed by a biodiesel producer and converted into a clean-burning renewable fuel used in diesel vehicles and equipment.

Oil must be free of water — even a small amount of water contamination can ruin an entire batch of biodiesel feedstock and cause the entire load to be rejected. Let oil cool and settle before bottling it for drop-off.

✓

Accepted — Cooking Oil

  • Used canola oil
  • Used vegetable oil (soybean, corn, sunflower)
  • Used coconut oil (liquid, not solidified)
  • Used olive oil and blended cooking oils
  • Used peanut oil
  • Fryer oil from home use
  • Sealed in clean, non-leaking containers
  • Household quantities (up to 5 gallons per visit)
✗

Not Accepted — Cooking Oil

  • Oil mixed with water (any amount)
  • Animal-based grease — lard, bacon drippings, tallow
  • Motor oil or automotive lubricants
  • Rancid oil with strong off-odors (may still be OK — ask staff)
  • Oil in open, leaking, or unlabeled containers
  • Large commercial quantities (contact a grease hauler)
  • Fish oil or seafood frying oil (high water content)
🧴How to Prepare Your Oil
  • Let used oil cool completely before handling
  • Pour into a clean, sealable container (original bottle, jug, or jar)
  • Use a fine strainer or cheesecloth to remove food particles
  • Wipe the rim and cap to prevent leaks in transport
  • Label the container "Used Cooking Oil"
  • Do not mix with any other liquids

🌱Environmental Benefit

Pouring cooking oil down the drain contributes to fatbergs — massive blockages of congealed fat and grease that damage sewer systems and eventually reach the ocean.

Every gallon of recycled cooking oil can produce approximately 1 gallon of biodiesel, replacing petroleum-based fuel with a renewable, lower-emission alternative.

🚫
Never pour cooking oil down the drain

Cooking oil solidifies in pipes and sewer lines, leading to costly blockages and overflows that can discharge raw sewage into streams and the ocean. Even "liquid" oils can solidify when they contact cooler surfaces in the plumbing system. Always collect and recycle used oil instead.
